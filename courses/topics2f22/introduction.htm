Introduction

The aim of these notes is to give the reader a sense of the breadth of mathematics, beyond the topics usually encountered through calculus. The notes were developed in connection with a year-long course offered to students who had recently completed a two-year calculus sequence. Very roughly, the topics were chosen by looking through the list of undergraduate courses offered at Penn State – there are several dozen – and trying to dedicate four or five hours to each major topic. We will not simply replicate the first week of each undergraduate class: instead, the goal for each chapter is to define a few basics, and then quickly get to a representative problem or theorem, without getting too bogged down in the details. This is not to say that there are no details here: many of the chapters contain proofs and careful derivations – in some cases more details than may be advisable – while other chapters are content with appeals to intuition.

Every chapter of the book includes many exercises for the reader, along with solutions. I encourage you to think about the problems, at least for a minute or two. There are also videos accompanying every lesson. Where possible, I have also included demonstrations written in the SageMath system. SageMath is essentially Python will added mathematical functionality, so if you have some programming experience I encourage you to experiment further!

The audience I had in mind when preparing these notes is advanced students who have studied calculus, both single- and multi-variable. However, most of the lessons do not actually require any multivariate calculus, and many of them require no calculus at all. Some of the lessons can be read independently of the rest of the notes, but some depend on the others. The chart on the next page gives a rough idea of the dependencies.

There are many sources aimed at introducing advanced mathematical topics to a general audience. Some of these are aimed at readers who have disliked math in the past, or haven’t looked at it in many years; these notes assume a strong fluency with precalculus and calculus, and are probably not the right place to start (but there are many excellent options; see here). Others introduce a variety of topics from the frontiers are current research; that’s not the goal here either, although we occasionally meet unsolved problems. Instead, the aim is to introduce the main topics from the undergraduate curriculum, while also providing plenty of opportunities for the reader to experiment and solve problems from a variety a fields.

Finally, a brief outline of the topics covered here:

  1. Cryptography (Diffie-Hellman key exchange, RSA, El-Gamal Digital signatures)
  2. Linear algebra with matrices (Linear maps, determinants, the Fibonacci formula)
  3. Knot theory (Reidemeister moves, knot invariants, Alexander polynomial)
  4. Counterexamples in real analysis (continuity, nowhere differentiable functions)
  5. Matrix computations (row reduction, LU decomposition, applications)
  6. Game theory (game trees, strategic form, Nash equilibrium)
  7. Factorization and primality testing (the Fermat pseudoprime test, Pollard rho, elliptic curve factorization)
  8. Optimization with calculus (critical points, Lagrange multipliers)
  9. Numerical optimization (Newton’s method, steepest descent)
  10. Dynamical systems (Bifurcation of the logistic map, Julia sets, Mandlebrot set)
  11. Inequalities
  12. Abstract linear algebra (vector spaces, inner product spaces, Fourier series)
  13. Differential equations (first- and second- order linear equations)
  14. Partial differential equations (the heat and wave equations)
  15. Random walks (recurrence in dimensions 1, 2, and 3, gambler’s ruin)
  16. Calculus of variations (Euler-Lagrange equation; the brachistochrone problem)
  17. Elliptic curves (the group law, cryptographic applications)
  18. Topology (homotopy, degree, fixed-point theorems, fundamental group, Dirac’s belt)
  19. Graph theory (basic facts, Dijkstra’s algorithm)

The specific topics and emphases necessarily reveal my own tastes and biases; I am an algebraist, and the examples chosen undoubtedly skew algebraic as well. While I’ve attempted to include some topics in applied math and other areas, this is far from my expertise and the exposition has suffered as a result.
